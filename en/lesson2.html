Noun roles - nV markers

Subject and Object

In kawaba, each nouns role in a sentence is indicated using a marker. Each marker starts with "n".

You may want to open Lesson 1 to use the root tables for these exercises!

{"ne"|subject marker} marks the subject of a sentence. In the English sentence "you eat something", "you" is the subject.

{"ne"|subject marker} always goes AFTER the noun that it is marking.

Try to translate these sentences using the root table in lesson 1:

{★ {da|I} {ne|subject marker} {pa|live}.| "I live." } ​ ​ {★ {ga|You} {ne|subject marker} {min|eat}.| "You eat." } ​ ​ {★ {pun|creature} {ne|subject marker} {ta|die}.| "A creature dies." }

{☆ Hover for more info | You may have noticed that tense and number are not specified. Therefore, valid translations would also be "I am living", "Creatures are dying", or any other variant.}

​

{"no"|object marker} marks the object of a sentence. In the English sentence "he eats pizza", "pizza" is the object. To remember {"no"|object marker} you can think that the "o" in it stands for "object".

{"no"|object marker} always goes BEFORE the noun that it is marking.

Try to translate these sentences:

{★ {fa|someone} {ne|subject marker} {wi|see} {no|object marker} {din|stars}.| "Someone sees the stars." } ​ ​ {★ {da|I} {ne|subject marker} {min|eat} {no|object marker} {je|this}.| "I eat this." } ​ ​ {★ {gun|man} {ne|subject marker} {cun|love} {no|object marker} {fun|woman}.| "A man loves a woman." }

You may have noticed that {"ne"|subject marker} and {"no"|subject marker} go on either side of the verb. This makes it easy to see which word in the sentence is the verb, and which are the nouns.

Topic, Location and Context

The markers {na|topic marker}, {ni|location marker}, and {nu|context marker} function very similarly to {ne|subject marker}, in that they go BEFORE the noun they mark.

In addition to this, words tagged with {na|topic marker}, {ni|location marker}, or {nu|context marker} must always appear at the start of a sentence, before the subject.

{na|topic marker} can be compared to the English words "as for". In the sentence "As for dinner, I ate with my father.", "dinner" is the topic. {na|topic marker} can also be read as a colon (:), "Dinner: I ate with my father.", or as "there is X such that..." (there is a dinner such that I ate with my father.)

{ni|location marker} can be compared to the English word "at" or "in". In the sentence "At school, I learn", "school" is the location.

{nu|context marker} can be compared to the English word "during". In the sentence "During the night, I sleep", "night" is the context. {nu|context marker} also has other uses that will be discussed at the end of this lesson.

Try to translate these sentences:

{★ {ha|true} {na|topic marker} {da|I} {ne|subject marker} {ho|inside} {no|object marker} {lin|house}.| "True: I am inside a house" } ​ ​ {★ {lin|house} {ni|location marker} {da|I} {ne|subject marker} {jon|play}.| "In the house, I play." }

{★ {pin|sky} {ni|location marker} {pun|creature} {ne|subject marker} {me|exists}.| "In the sky, a creature exists." } {★ {pa|life} {nu|context marker} {ca|thing} {na|topic marker} {da|I} {ne|subject marker} {cun|love}.| "During life, there is a thing that I love." }
